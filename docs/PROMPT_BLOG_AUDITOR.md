Every article already serves these strategic purposes. **Do NOT remove or dilute them** — only strengthen:

3. **Patient Education & Trust** — Articles convert strangers into patients by demonstrating clinical reasoning. The tone is direct, opinionated, and confident — like a clinician explaining to a smart patient. Do not make it academic or bland.
4. **Conversion** — Each article has a CTA (Book Assessment). Preserve or strengthen the conversion path.
5. **Shareability** — Articles are designed to be shared by athletes with teammates, coaches, and parents. The engaging, myth-busting angle is intentional.

### SEARCH-INTENT OVERRIDE (CRITICAL)

Right Track blog articles are **not magazine features**. They are search-first assets that may also be engaging.

This means:
- The **primary keyword / question must be obvious in the title, H1, intro, and subheadings**
- Curiosity-driven, witty, or editorial phrasing is allowed **only after** the search intent is made explicit
- If a title sounds clickable but does **not** clearly match the query a patient would type into Google, treat it as a weakness
- When in doubt, choose **clarity over cleverness**

Examples:
- Better: `Office Ergonomics for Neck and Back Pain: What Actually Helps`
- Worse: `Why Your Desk Setup Is Causing Pain`
- Better: `ACL Recovery Timeline for Athletes: What to Expect After Surgery`
- Worse: `What 90% of Athletes Get Wrong About Recovery`
- Better: `GESY Physiotherapy in Cyprus: Referral, Sessions, Cost, Coverage`
- Worse: `Everything You Need to Know About GESY`

## WHAT TO AUDIT (YOUR DELIVERABLES)

### 1. EVIDENCE AUDIT

**Current state:** The articles reference research generally (e.g., "studies show," "research consistently demonstrates," "a major systematic review found") but **do not cite specific sources with proper credentials**.

**Your task:**
- Identify every clinical claim in the article that requires evidence support
- For each claim, find and provide a **real, verifiable reference** from:
  - **PubMed** (peer-reviewed journals: BJSM, JOSPT, AJSM, Knee Surgery Sports Traumatology Arthroscopy, etc.)
  - **BMJ** / **The Lancet** / **NEJM** (for high-impact findings)
  - **Cochrane Reviews** (for systematic review claims)
  - **Consensus.app** (medical AI search engine) — use this to cross-verify claims and find the most cited/relevant papers. When citing a source found via Consensus, cite the **original paper**, not Consensus itself.
- For each reference provide:
  - Full author list (or First Author et al.)
  - Title
  - Journal
  - Year
  - DOI or PMID when available
- Flag any claims that are **not supported** by current evidence or are **outdated**
- **CRITICAL: Flag any citations older than 10 years** (before 2016 as of 2026). These MUST be replaced with modern equivalents. No exceptions — outdated citations undermine clinical credibility.
- Flag any claims where the evidence is **stronger than stated** (opportunity to be more authoritative)

**Format for each claim:**

```
CLAIM: "Athletes who return before 9 months have a significantly higher re-injury rate"
STATUS: ✅ Supported
SOURCE: Grindem H, et al. "Simple decision rules can reduce reinjury risk by 84% after ACL reconstruction." BJSM. 2016;50(13):804-808. DOI: 10.1136/bjsports-2016-096031
NOTE: The evidence is actually stronger than stated — Grindem found 84% risk reduction per month of delayed return. Consider strengthening the claim.
```

```
CLAIM: "Nordic hamstring curls reduce injury risk by up to 51%"
STATUS: ✅ Supported
SOURCE: van Dyk N, et al. "Including the Nordic hamstring exercise in injury prevention programmes halves the rate of hamstring injuries." BJSM. 2019;53(21):1362-1370. DOI: 10.1136/bjsports-2018-100045
NOTE: Accurate. Could add that the effect is dose-dependent.
```

### 3. REFERENCE INTEGRATION RECOMMENDATIONS

Suggest **how** to integrate citations into the article without destroying readability. Two approaches:

**Option A: Inline Contextual Citations (Recommended for this blog)**
Instead of academic footnotes, weave references naturally:
> *"Research from the British Journal of Sports Medicine shows that each month of delayed return after ACL reconstruction reduces re-injury risk by up to 84% (Grindem et al., 2016)."*

**Option B: "Evidence Box" Callout**
A styled callout box at the end of key sections:
> **What does the research say?** This recommendation is based on [Study Name], published in [Journal] ([Year]), which found [key finding]. [Link to PubMed/DOI]

**Option C: Dedicated References Section**
A "Sources & Further Reading" section at the bottom of the article, before the author box. This is the most SEO-friendly approach as it creates an E-E-A-T signal (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) for Google.

**Recommend which option (or combination) is best for each specific article.**

## HOW TO USE CONSENSUS (consensus.app)

When verifying claims or finding sources:

1. Search Consensus for the specific clinical claim (e.g., "ACL reconstruction return to sport timing reinjury rate")
2. Review the synthesized answer AND the underlying papers
3. Always cite the **original published paper**, not Consensus
4. Use Consensus to check if there are **more recent or higher-impact papers** than the ones commonly cited
5. If Consensus shows a scientific consensus meter, note the confidence level (e.g., "Strong consensus: Yes, supported by 9/10 studies")

2. **DO NOT** make the writing academic or jargon-heavy — the audience is athletes, not researchers
